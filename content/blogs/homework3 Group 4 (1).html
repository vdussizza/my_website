Confidence Interval

Boxplots show how the medians of the two distributions compare, but we can also compare the means of the distributions using either a confidence interval or a hypothesis test. Note that when we calculate the mean, SD, etc. weight in these groups using the mean function, we must ignore any missing values by setting the na.rm = TRUE.

#Construct a 95% CI to see whether there is a difference of physical activity in weight
formula_ci <- yrbss %>% 
  select(physical_3plus,weight) %>% 
  filter(!is.na(weight)) %>% 
  group_by(physical_3plus) %>% 
  summarise(mean=mean(weight,na.rm = TRUE),
            sd=sd(weight,na.rm = TRUE),
            count=n(),
            t_critical=qt(0.975,count-1),
            SE=sd/sqrt(count),
            margin_of_error=t_critical*SE,
            rating_low=round(mean-margin_of_error,2),
            rating_high=round(mean+margin_of_error,2))

There is an observed difference of about 1.77kg (68.44 - 66.67), and we notice that the two confidence intervals do not overlap. It seems that the difference is at least 95% statistically significant. Let us also conduct a hypothesis test.

Hypothesis test with infer

Next, we will introduce a new function, hypothesize, that falls into the infer workflow. You will use this method for conducting hypothesis tests.

But first, we need to initialize the test, which we will save as obs_diff.

obs_diff <- yrbss %>%
  specify(weight ~ physical_3plus) %>%
  calculate(stat = "diff in means", order = c("yes", "no"))

Notice how you can use the functions specify and calculate again like you did for calculating confidence intervals. Here, though, the statistic you are searching for is the difference in means, with the order being yes - no != 0.

After you have initialized the test, you need to simulate the test on the null distribution, which we will save as null.

null_dist <- yrbss %>%
  # specify variables
  specify(weight ~ physical_3plus) %>%
  
  # assume independence, i.e, there is no difference
  hypothesize(null = "independence") %>%
  
  # generate 1000 reps, of type "permute"
  generate(reps = 1000, type = "permute") %>%
  
  # calculate statistic of difference, namely "diff in means"
  calculate(stat = "diff in means", order = c("yes", "no"))

Here, hypothesize is used to set the null hypothesis as a test for independence, i.e., that there is no difference between the two population means. In one sample cases, the null argument can be set to point to test a hypothesis relative to a point estimate.

Also, note that the type argument within generate is set to permute, which is the argument when generating a null distribution for a hypothesis test.

We can visualize this null distribution with the following code:

ggplot(data = null_dist, aes(x = stat)) +
  geom_histogram()

Now that the test is initialized and the null distribution formed, we can visualise to see how many of these null permutations have a difference of at least obs_stat of 1.77?

We can also calculate the p-value for your hypothesis test using the function infer::get_p_value().

null_dist %>% visualize() +
  shade_p_value(obs_stat = obs_diff, direction = "two-sided")

What is this last chart telling you? Can you explain in a couple of paragraphs the different dynamic among these three countries?

The primary difference between the three countries is the state of economic development. Germany and the United States are developed economies, while India is a developing economy.

The level of Household Expenditure for Germany and the United States has been relatively stable as a proportion to the total GDP at approximately 60%. Meanwhile, India starkly contrasts with these two countries, as the proportion of its Household Expenditures has decreased from 82% to 55% over 50 years. The change started in 1990, when the Indian Economy was liberalized and became a more business-friendly nation, where individuals and companies saw more opportunities and diverted capital from Consumption to Investment (GCF).

In terms of GCF, Germany has experienced a decrease in proposition by almost 10% over the last 50 years. This can be attributed to the fact that post-WW2, Germany was heaving investing into the reconstruction of its economy. After the late 1990s, when East Germany was reunified with West Germany, Investment decreased to approximately 20%, about the same level as the United States, signalling that it became a healthy economy. The United States, on the other hand, has had relatively stable investment, excluding dips in investment due to business cycles, specifically economic boom in the early 2000s and the 2009 financial crisis.

Government expenditure differs by country, however, has remained relatively stable over the last 50 years. Every country has its own expectations of growth rate, and generally are able to manage their government budget as an expected percentage of GDP. Additionally, interest rates differed greatly across countries. In developing countries like India, the government’s borrowing costs were high, particularly pre-1990s. Thus, the Indian government had to be prudent in their spending patterns. With the United States being the heart and soul of capitalism, over the years, more power has been transferred to markets. Thus, this may be the reason that government expenditure has been falling in the US.

Lastly, the level of net exports, defined as exports minus imports has varied across countries. In Germany, the proportion of net exports has increased significantly after the 2000s, starting from being a balanced importing and exporting nation to becoming an export-driven nation. It is possible that this change occurred due to increased goods mobility in the EU. Both India and the United States, on the other hand, have become predominantly net importers over the last few decades. This implies that the countries import a greater value of goods than they export. In India, this can be attributed to the market liberalization. Meanwhile, the United States has the largest goods consumption in the world, which explains a growing influx in imports in recent years.
